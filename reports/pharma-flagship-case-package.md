## PART 3 — Why Torcetrapib Is the Strongest Case

### The Argument in One Sentence

Pfizer had a published safety signal, $800M invested, a Lipitor patent cliff creating financial pressure, and a council that would have said WAIT — and they advanced anyway, killing 82 people and destroying $21B in market capitalisation.

### Eight Reasons It Is the Strongest Case

**1. Public recognizability (10/10).** Torcetrapib is one of the most cited pharmaceutical failures in the academic literature. Every head of clinical development at a top-20 pharma company knows this case. No explanation required. The name alone creates credibility.

**2. Availability of evidence (10/10).** The pre-decision evidence is entirely public. The Phase II blood pressure signal was published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* in 2004 (Brousseau et al.). The CETP mechanism was published in 2000. The Phase II trial results were disclosed in Pfizer's 2004 annual report. The ILLUMINATE trial design was registered on ClinicalTrials.gov in 2004. Every document required to build the evidence manifest exists in the public domain with DOIs.

**3. Governance failure visibility (10/10).** The governance failure is specific, documented, and non-controversial. The Phase II blood pressure signal (+2 mmHg systolic) was published. Pfizer's internal review acknowledged it. The decision to advance was made despite the signal. The ILLUMINATE Data Safety Monitoring Board terminated the trial in December 2006 citing excess mortality. The causal mechanism — off-target aldosterone stimulation — was confirmed by Forrest et al. in 2008. There is no ambiguity about what happened or why.

**4. Financial impact (10/10).** $800M in direct write-off. $21B in market capitalisation lost in a single trading session on December 4, 2006. Pfizer's stock fell 10.6% in one day. These numbers are verifiable, public, and large enough to command the attention of any CFO, portfolio manager, or investment committee.

**5. Regulatory significance (9/10).** The case predates the FDA's enhanced cardiovascular safety requirements for lipid-modifying agents. The ILLUMINATE termination directly influenced the FDA's subsequent guidance on cardiovascular endpoint requirements for this drug class. The regulatory context is rich and documented.

**6. Ease of explanation (10/10).** The case can be explained to a non-scientist in three sentences: *"Pfizer was developing a drug to raise good cholesterol. In Phase II trials, the drug also raised blood pressure. Pfizer advanced to Phase III anyway. 82 people died. The company lost $21 billion in one day."* No scientific background required.

**7. Council value demonstration (10/10).** The council verdict (WAIT, 0/8/2) is retrospectively correct. The primary blocker — the unexplained blood pressure signal — is exactly the signal that caused the Phase III termination. The council identified the right risk, for the right reason, using only pre-decision evidence. This is the clearest possible demonstration of council value.

**8. Scrutiny resistance (9/10).** The one vulnerability is the LLM contamination risk: the model may have been trained on post-failure data. This must be disclosed. However, the evidence boundary is the most thoroughly documented of any case in the library (23 admitted documents, all with DOIs, all pre-December 2005). The case can withstand expert review better than any other case in the library, provided the LLM limitation is disclosed upfront.

### The One Weakness

The LLM contamination risk is real and must be disclosed in every presentation. The exact language: *"The council was run using a large language model. We cannot verify that the model's training data did not include post-failure information about torcetrapib. We have documented the evidence provided to the council. The evidence boundary is available for independent review."*

This disclosure does not destroy the case. It demonstrates intellectual honesty, which is itself a credibility signal.

## Appendix — Flagship Case Selection Rationale

The decision to select torcetrapib over sofosbuvir (the second-ranked case) is based on the following logic:

Sofosbuvir is a success case. The council issued a clean GO. The drug was approved and generated $12.4B in revenue. This is an important case for demonstrating that the council does not produce false-positive blocking. However, it is not the right flagship case.

The flagship case must create urgency. Urgency comes from failure, not success. A pharma executive who sees the torcetrapib case understands immediately what the governance gap costs. A pharma executive who sees the sofosbuvir case thinks: *"We already have a process for identifying good drugs."*

The torcetrapib case answers the question that matters: *"What would this council have done with a drug that killed people?"* The answer is: it would have said WAIT. That is the case that opens doors.

Sofosbuvir should be the second case shown — immediately after torcetrapib — to demonstrate that the council does not block good drugs. The two cases together tell the complete story: the council stops failures and approves successes.
