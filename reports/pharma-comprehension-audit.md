# AgenThink Pharma — Blind-Comprehension Audit
## /pharma Route — First-Time Visitor Experience Review

**Audit Date:** June 16, 2026  
**Auditor:** Independent Usability Review (Simulated)  
**Scope:** Live /pharma route — comprehension, clarity, credibility, conversion  
**Method:** Five independent reviewer simulations reading the page top to bottom  
**Verdict:** Stated first, evidence follows

---

## VERDICT

> **The page is credible to someone who already understands what AgenThink does. It is incomprehensible to someone who does not.**

The page is built for an audience that already knows what a "council," a "WAIT verdict," an "OVS score," and an "Institutional Proof Report" are. That audience does not exist yet. Every first-time visitor from Recursion, Insilico, Medpace, ICON, or IQVIA will arrive without that vocabulary. The page will lose them in the first 30 seconds — not because the content is wrong, but because the entry point assumes prior knowledge that has not been established.

The single most important fix is not a design change. It is a vocabulary change: replace internal terminology with plain English before the visitor has been given a reason to care.

## PART 4 — Conversion Killers

The following 10 factors are the most likely reasons a prospect leaves without requesting a meeting, ranked by evidence from the reviewer simulations above.

**1. No value proposition statement in the hero.**
The hero tells the torcetrapib story but never states what AgenThink does. A visitor who does not already know the company leaves the hero section without understanding the product. Evidence: all five reviewers formed incomplete hypotheses at 60 seconds.

**2. "OVS" is used before it is defined.**
The term appears in the stats bar (the second element on the page), in the Validation Library table header, and in the Torcetrapib Deep Dive — three times before any definition. Visitors who cannot interpret a key metric lose confidence in the page. Evidence: Reviewer 1 (CEO) and Reviewer 5 (non-pharma) both flagged this explicitly.

**3. The disclosure strip creates a negative first impression for regulatory professionals.**
"Not for regulatory use" is the first text a regulatory professional reads. It immediately categorises the product as out-of-scope. Evidence: Reviewer 3 (Regulatory Affairs Director) had a 20% meeting probability — the lowest of any reviewer — and attributed it directly to this framing.

**4. The governance benefit is buried in Section 5.**
The most differentiated claim — "the value is governance enforcement, not signal identification" — appears in the FAQ and in the Governance Layer section, which is the fifth section on the page. Most visitors disengage before reaching it. Evidence: all five reviewers spent less than 20 seconds on the Governance Layer.

**5. "The Council" is never introduced before it is used.**
The headline assumes the visitor knows what "the Council" is. The concept is not explained until Section 4 (Council Chamber), which is the fourth section on a long page. Evidence: Reviewer 1 (CEO) and Reviewer 5 (non-pharma) both formed incomplete hypotheses because the council concept was not established.

**6. The Validation Library table is dense and unexplained.**
The table has six columns (Verdict, Votes, OVS, Outcome, Alignment, Financial Impact) with no legend, no tooltip, and no introductory sentence. Visitors who cannot read the table skip it. Evidence: Reviewer 5 (non-pharma) scrolled past without reading it.

**7. The sample size objection is not pre-empted.**
"10 cases is not statistically significant" is the most common objection from clinical and statistical professionals. It is answered in the FAQ (the last section) but not pre-empted in the Validation Library where the data is presented. Evidence: Reviewer 2 (Head of Clinical) thought "that's not a strong enough track record" at 30 seconds.

**8. The pilot proposal headline describes format, not benefit.**
"30-Day Retrospective Pilot" tells the visitor what the format is. It does not tell them what they will learn, what decision they will be able to make, or why the 30 days is worth $25,000. Evidence: Reviewer 4 (CRO) read the deliverables list before reading the headline — they were looking for the benefit, not the format.

**9. The LLM limitation disclosure is placed inside the governance section.**
The most important credibility statement on the page — "we cannot fully override the training cutoff with a prompt instruction" — is buried inside a technical section that most visitors do not read carefully. Visitors who do read it may be alarmed by the admission without the context needed to evaluate it. Evidence: Reviewer 3 (Regulatory) found it "either very honest or very concerning."

**10. There is no social proof, no named client, and no named advisor.**
The page has no testimonials, no named partners, no advisory board members, and no institutional affiliations. For a product asking for $25,000 and access to proprietary drug development data, the absence of any third-party credibility signal is a significant barrier. Evidence: all five reviewers implicitly noted the absence — none of them mentioned a reason to trust the company beyond the quality of the content itself.

## PART 5 — Message Simplification

### Current Hero Headline
> "The Council Said WAIT."

**Problem:** Assumes the visitor knows what "the Council" is. Leads with internal terminology.

**5 Alternative Versions:**

1. *"In 2005, a structured review would have stopped Pfizer's biggest failure."*
   — Plain English. Specific. Credible. Leads with the outcome, not the product.

2. *"We run a 10-expert review of your Phase II drug before you commit to Phase III."*
   — States the product clearly. No jargon. Immediately comprehensible.

3. *"82 deaths. $21B lost. A structured governance process would have said no."*
   — Leads with consequence. Creates urgency. Explains the value in one sentence.

4. *"Phase II → Phase III is the most expensive decision in drug development. We help you make it with a documented, auditable, multi-expert review."*
   — Functional. Clear. Positions the product as a process improvement, not a technology.

5. *"Pfizer had the data. They advanced anyway. We build the governance process that makes that harder to do."*
   — Conversational. Honest. Positions the product as a governance solution, not an AI tool.

---

### Current Hero Subheadline
> "Torcetrapib. Phase III advanced. 82 deaths. $21B lost."

**Problem:** Factual but context-free. Does not explain what AgenThink did or would have done.

**5 Alternative Versions:**

1. *"A Phase II blood pressure signal was dismissed. A structured 10-expert review — bounded to pre-failure evidence — issued a unanimous WAIT verdict. The council was right."*
   — Explains the product, the method, and the outcome in three sentences.

2. *"The Phase II data contained a warning. Pfizer's governance process missed it. Ours would not have."*
   — Direct. Confident. Explains the differentiation without jargon.

3. *"AgenThink runs retrospective governance reviews of historical drug development decisions — using only the evidence that existed at the time. The torcetrapib council said WAIT. Pfizer advanced. 82 people died."*
   — Full context. No jargon. Explains the evidence boundary without using the term.

4. *"We rebuilt the Pfizer decision committee using only 2005 evidence. Ten independent reviewers. Zero GO votes. The drug was advanced anyway."*
   — Vivid. Specific. Explains the methodology without technical language.

5. *"A 10-expert review of the torcetrapib Phase II data — using only evidence available before the Phase III decision — produced a unanimous recommendation to wait. That recommendation was correct."*
   — Formal. Credible. Appropriate for a pharma executive audience.

---

### Current Pilot Proposal Headline
> "30-Day Retrospective Pilot"

**Problem:** Describes the format. Does not state the benefit.

**5 Alternative Versions:**

1. *"Run a governance review on your own historical drug decision."*
   — States the action and the ownership. No jargon.

2. *"Find out what a structured 10-expert council would have said about your Phase II decision — before you knew the outcome."*
   — Explains the value. Creates curiosity. Appropriate for clinical and R&D audiences.

3. *"Test the methodology on a drug you already know the answer to."*
   — Disarmingly simple. Removes the risk from the buyer's perspective.

4. *"One drug. Thirty days. A documented governance record you can use internally."*
   — Specific. Practical. Focuses on the deliverable, not the format.

5. *"Validate the process before you trust it with a live decision."*
   — Acknowledges the buyer's scepticism. Frames the pilot as a risk-reduction exercise.

## Summary

The /pharma page has strong content and weak communication. The torcetrapib story is compelling. The data is honest. The limitations are disclosed. The governance architecture is real. None of this matters if the visitor cannot understand what the product does within the first 30 seconds.

The 10 fixes above require approximately 10 hours of work and zero new features. They address the comprehension failures that are currently preventing conversion. The most important single change — adding a one-sentence value proposition to the hero — takes one hour and would increase the 5-minute comprehension score from 4.5 to an estimated 6.5.

The page is not broken. It is speaking to an audience that does not yet exist. The fixes above make it speak to the audience that does.
