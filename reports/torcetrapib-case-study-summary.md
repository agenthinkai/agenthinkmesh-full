## THE BET

For torcetrapib to succeed in Phase III, the +2 mmHg systolic BP increase observed in Phase II must be either (a) a statistical artifact, (b) a manageable class effect shared by all CETP inhibitors, or (c) offset by the HDL-C benefit in the high-CV-risk population. None of these conditions were established before Pfizer advanced.

10 of 10 council members voted WAIT or NO-GO. The primary blocker is the unresolved systolic blood pressure signal (+2 mmHg) in the Phase II dataset, which requires mechanistic investigation before enrolling 15,000 high-cardiovascular-risk patients in Phase III.

## 3 Reasons the Council Said WAIT / NO-GO

**1. Unresolved BP Signal — No Mechanistic Explanation**
Brousseau et al. NEJM 2004 (Table 3) documented a statistically significant +2 mmHg systolic BP increase at the 60mg dose. Pfizer characterized it as "small but statistically significant." The council applied Lewington et al. Lancet 2002: each 2 mmHg systolic BP increase = 7% stroke mortality increase + 4% ischemic heart disease mortality increase. In a 15,000-patient high-CV-risk Phase III population, this is not a small signal — it is the primary outcome driver.

**2. Molecule-Specific Signal — Not a Class Effect**
No competing CETP inhibitor (dalcetrapib, anacetrapib) showed a comparable BP signal in available Phase I/II data as of December 31, 2005. This means the BP effect is likely torcetrapib-specific, not a CETP class effect. A molecule-specific unexplained BP increase in a cardiovascular drug is a hard blocker under PC-007 (Mechanistic Investigation Requirement).

**3. Surrogate Endpoint Risk — HDL-C Not Validated for CV Benefit**
HDL-C increase is a surrogate endpoint. The validated pathway from HDL-C increase to cardiovascular event reduction is not established. If the BP increase offsets or reverses the HDL benefit, the trial will show net harm — which is exactly what ILLUMINATE demonstrated.

## What Would Change the Decision

The council identified four conditions that would allow advancement to Phase III:

| Requirement | Rule | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanistic study: is BP increase on-target (CETP) or off-target (torcetrapib-specific)? | PC-007 | Not completed before Phase III |
| DSMB charter amendment: explicit BP stopping rules | PC-008 | Not in ILLUMINATE charter |
| Competitive class comparison: BP data from dalcetrapib and anacetrapib | PC-005 | Not obtained before Phase III |
| Quantitative risk-benefit analysis: BP harm vs HDL benefit in Phase III population | PC-006 | Not commissioned before Phase III |

None of these conditions were met before Pfizer advanced torcetrapib to Phase III.

The Forrest et al. 2008 publication confirmed that torcetrapib stimulates aldosterone secretion via an off-target mechanism independent of CETP inhibition. This is exactly the molecule-specific off-target effect the council flagged as the primary blocker. A mechanistic investigation before Phase III would have identified this mechanism and prevented the trial.
