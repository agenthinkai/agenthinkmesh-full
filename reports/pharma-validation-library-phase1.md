## Prefatory Note

This document does not make promotional claims. It presents a methodology, a ranked corpus, and a statistical framework for evaluating whether a governed multi-persona council — operating exclusively on pre-decision evidence — consistently identifies the key risk signals, opportunity signals, and decision drivers that were visible before major pharmaceutical outcomes became known.

The standard applied throughout is scientific defensibility, not commercial utility. Every claim in this document is grounded in published literature, regulatory filings, or documented financial disclosures. Where evidence is incomplete, that incompleteness is stated explicitly.

The audience for this work is assumed to be skeptical. That skepticism is appropriate. The value of retrospective validation is proportional to the rigor of its methodology, and rigor requires honesty about what the data can and cannot prove.

## Executive Summary

Phase II to Phase III advancement decisions are among the most consequential and least governed decisions in pharmaceutical R&D. The average Phase III failure rate for drugs that reach Phase III is approximately 40–50%, with cardiovascular and neurological indications performing substantially worse. The estimated cost of a failed Phase III program ranges from $300M to $2B in direct R&D expenditure, with market capitalization impacts that can reach $20B or more for large-cap companies.

This report identifies 20 historical Phase II to Phase III advancement decisions suitable for retrospective council analysis, ranks them by suitability, recommends the 10 highest-priority cases for immediate execution, and defines the statistical framework required before any public claims about council accuracy can be made.

The central question is not whether a governed council would have made the right call in every case. The central question is whether a governed council, operating on pre-decision evidence, would have identified the key signals that determined the outcome. That is a testable, falsifiable claim — and this report defines the methodology for testing it.

## Deliverable C — Pharma Validation Scorecard

### Purpose

The Pharma Validation Scorecard provides a standardised framework for evaluating the accuracy of each retrospective council run. It is designed to produce a single, auditable score that can be compared across cases and used to assess the statistical validity of the council's signal detection capability.

The scorecard is not a marketing instrument. It is a scientific measurement tool. Every dimension is defined operationally, with explicit criteria for each score level.

### Scorecard Dimensions

**Dimension 1: Council Verdict Accuracy (CVA)**

Definition: Did the council's verdict (GO / WAIT / NO-GO) match the retrospectively correct decision?

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 10 | Council verdict exactly matches retrospectively correct decision |
| 7 | Council verdict partially correct (e.g., WAIT when NO-GO was correct, but key blocker identified) |
| 4 | Council verdict incorrect but key signal identified in rationale |
| 0 | Council verdict incorrect and key signal not identified |

**Dimension 2: Actual Outcome Alignment (AOA)**

Definition: How well did the council's predicted outcome align with the actual Phase III outcome?

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 10 | Council predicted the specific failure mode or success mechanism |
| 7 | Council predicted the direction (failure/success) but not the specific mechanism |
| 4 | Council predicted mixed outcome |
| 0 | Council predicted opposite direction |

**Dimension 3: Signal Detection Accuracy (SDA)**

Definition: What fraction of the key pre-decision signals did the council identify?

Calculated as: (Signals correctly identified / Total key signals present) × 10

Key signals are defined as those documented in the primary Phase II publication or regulatory filing.

**Dimension 4: Correct Blocker Identification (CBI)**

Definition: For failure cases — did the council identify the specific mechanism that caused Phase III failure? For success cases — did the council identify the specific mechanism that drove Phase III success?

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 10 | Primary blocker/driver correctly identified with mechanistic specificity |
| 7 | Primary blocker/driver identified at category level (e.g., "safety signal" without specific mechanism) |
| 4 | Secondary blocker/driver identified; primary missed |
| 0 | No relevant blocker/driver identified |

**Dimension 5: False Positive Blockers (FPB)**

Definition: For success cases — did the council raise blockers that would have incorrectly prevented a successful drug from advancing?

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 10 | No false positive blockers raised |
| 7 | Minor false positive blockers raised but not decision-determinative |
| 4 | Significant false positive blockers raised that would have delayed advancement |
| 0 | False positive blockers would have prevented advancement of a successful drug |

**Dimension 6: Missed Risks (MR)**

Definition: What fraction of the key risk signals present in Phase II data did the council fail to identify?

Calculated as: (1 - (Missed risks / Total risks present)) × 10

**Dimension 7: Governance Quality (GQ)**

Definition: Did the council apply the correct constitutional rules? Were the right personas most influential? Was the deliberation process sound?

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 10 | All relevant constitutional rules applied; correct personas drove the decision; deliberation was sound |
| 7 | Most relevant rules applied; minor gaps in persona influence |
| 4 | Some rules applied; significant gaps |
| 0 | Constitutional rules not applied; deliberation unsound |

**Dimension 8: Evidence Completeness (EC)**

Definition: Did the council use all available pre-decision evidence? Were there material evidence gaps?

| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 10 | All material pre-decision evidence used; no gaps |
| 7 | Most material evidence used; minor gaps |
| 4 | Significant evidence gaps |
| 0 | Material evidence not used |

**Dimension 9: Retrospective Validation Score (RVS)**

Definition: Composite score — weighted average of all eight dimensions.

Weighting:

| Dimension | Weight |
|---|---|
| Council Verdict Accuracy | 20% |
| Signal Detection Accuracy | 20% |
| Correct Blocker Identification | 20% |
| False Positive Blockers | 15% |
| Missed Risks | 10% |
| Governance Quality | 5% |
| Evidence Completeness | 5% |
| Actual Outcome Alignment | 5% |

**RVS = (CVA × 0.20) + (SDA × 0.20) + (CBI × 0.20) + (FPB × 0.15) + (MR × 0.10) + (GQ × 0.05) + (EC × 0.05) + (AOA × 0.05)**

## Deliverable D — Validation Roadmap

### Execution Sequence

The 10 cases are sequenced to maximise learning velocity and statistical coverage. The sequence moves from the highest-confidence cases (where council accuracy is most likely) to the most challenging cases (where council accuracy is most uncertain), building a credible track record before testing edge cases.

### Case 1: Torcetrapib — COMPLETED

**Why selected:** Highest composite score among failure cases. Clean pre-decision evidence. BP signal is mechanistically specific and epidemiologically quantifiable. Serves as the anchor case for all subsequent validation.

**What we expect to learn:** Whether the council correctly identifies a specific, quantifiable safety signal as a Phase III blocker when financial pressure to advance is documented and the company characterised the signal as "small."

**Personas most tested:** Drug Safety Expert, Chief Biostatistician, Clinical Pharmacologist.

**Constitutional rules most challenged:** PC-002 (Safety Signal Priority), PC-003 (Financial Independence), PC-007 (Mechanistic Investigation Requirement).

**Result:** WAIT — 0 GO / 8 WAIT / 2 NO-GO. RVS: 9.8/10. Retrospectively correct.

**What we expect to learn:** Whether the council correctly identifies a cognitive worsening signal as a hard blocker in an Alzheimer's disease indication, where the patient population is the most vulnerable to cognitive harm.

**Personas most tested:** Drug Safety Expert, Patient Advocate, Clinical Pharmacologist.

**Why selected:** Dual PPAR agonist with cardiovascular safety signal in Phase II. FDA advisory committee flagged the signal. Drug was withdrawn pre-approval. **What we expect to learn:** Whether the council identifies a cardiovascular safety signal in a non-cardiovascular indication (type 2 diabetes), where the signal may be characterised as an incidental finding rather than a primary concern.

**Personas most tested:** Regulatory Strategist, Drug Safety Expert, Devil's Advocate.

**Constitutional rules most challenged:** PC-002 (Safety Signal Priority), PC-005 (Competitive Class Analysis), PC-009 (Regulatory Risk Documentation).

### Case 4: Sofosbuvir (Gilead, 2012)

**Why selected:** Transformative HCV cure with exceptional Phase II data. Tests the council's ability to identify a breakthrough signal and avoid false-positive blocking. The risk in this case is not a safety signal — it is the risk of over-caution in a high-unmet-need indication.

**What we expect to learn:** Whether the council correctly issues a GO verdict for a drug with clean Phase II data, strong mechanistic rationale, and high unmet need — without raising false-positive blockers that would have delayed a transformative therapy.

**Constitutional rules most challenged:** PC-001 (Evidence Primacy), PC-004 (Surrogate Endpoint Scrutiny — in this case, SVR12 as a surrogate for cure).

**Constitutional rules most challenged:** PC-001 (Evidence Primacy — small dataset), PC-004 (Surrogate Endpoint — ORR as surrogate for OS), PC-009 (Regulatory Risk Documentation).

## Deliverable E — Statistical Credibility Assessment

### Prefatory Caution

This section is written for an audience that includes FDA reviewers, EMA reviewers, and academic statisticians. The standards applied are those of peer-reviewed clinical research, not those of commercial validation. Claims are graded by the level of evidence they require, and the minimum case count for each claim level is stated explicitly.

The fundamental challenge is that retrospective validation is not a randomised controlled trial. The council cannot be blinded to the drug name, company, and therapeutic area — all of which carry information about the likely outcome. This is a structural limitation of retrospective validation that cannot be fully resolved. It can be mitigated through methodology (evidence cutoff enforcement, persona independence, constitutional rule pre-commitment), but it cannot be eliminated.

Any claims made on the basis of retrospective validation must acknowledge this limitation explicitly.

### Statistical Framework

**Base Rate Context**

The relevant base rate for council accuracy is not 50% (random guessing). The relevant base rate is the accuracy of the actual Phase II to III advancement decisions made by the companies in this corpus. Of the 20 cases in this library:

- 8 were blockbuster launches (companies were correct to advance)
- 4 were major failures or terminated programs where the company advanced despite visible warning signals (companies were incorrect to advance)
- 3 were controversial approvals (outcome is contested)
- 3 were regulatory reversals or edge cases (outcome is mixed)
- 2 were major failures with no visible pre-decision warning signals (companies were correct to advance given available evidence)

The base rate for "company made the correct advancement decision given available pre-decision evidence" is approximately 50–60% across this corpus. A council that simply replicated the company's decision would score 50–60% accuracy. The council must demonstrably outperform this base rate to make a credible claim.

### Credibility Thresholds by Case Count

**After 1 case (current status: Torcetrapib completed)**

Defensible claims:
- "We have run one retrospective pilot. The council issued a WAIT verdict on torcetrapib. The actual outcome was a Phase III termination for excess mortality. The council's primary blocker (unresolved BP signal) was confirmed as the cause of failure by Forrest et al. 2008."
- "This is a proof of concept, not a validation. One case proves that the methodology can be applied. It does not prove that the methodology is accurate."

Non-defensible claims:
- Any claim about accuracy rates, sensitivity, or specificity
- Any claim about the council's ability to generalise across therapeutic areas or decision types

**After 5 cases**

Defensible claims:
- Descriptive statistics: "The council correctly identified the primary blocker in X of 5 cases."
- Directional accuracy: "The council issued the retrospectively correct verdict (GO/WAIT/NO-GO) in X of 5 cases."
- Qualitative pattern: "Across 5 cases, the council consistently [identified safety signals / avoided false-positive blocking / applied constitutional rules]."

Non-defensible claims:
- Statistical significance (5 cases is insufficient for any inferential statistics)
- Generalisation to untested therapeutic areas
- Comparison to industry base rates (requires matched controls)

**After 10 cases**

Defensible claims:
- Point estimates with confidence intervals: "The council correctly identified the primary blocker in 8 of 10 cases (80%, 95% CI: 44–97%)." Note: confidence intervals at n=10 are very wide.
- Subgroup patterns: "In failure cases (n=4), the council correctly identified the primary blocker in all 4 cases. In success cases (n=4), the council issued a GO verdict without false-positive blocking in 3 of 4 cases."
- Comparison to a stated base rate: "The company's actual advancement decision was retrospectively correct in 6 of 10 cases (60%). The council's verdict was retrospectively correct in 8 of 10 cases (80%)."

Non-defensible claims:
- Statistical significance (p < 0.05 requires approximately 20+ cases for a meaningful test)
- Claims about specific therapeutic areas (subgroup sizes are too small)
- Claims about prospective accuracy (retrospective validation does not prove prospective accuracy)

**After 20 cases**

Defensible claims:
- Statistical significance: With 20 cases, a binomial test can detect accuracy rates significantly above 50% with reasonable power. If the council achieves 80% accuracy (16/20), the p-value against a 50% null hypothesis is approximately 0.006.
- Subgroup analysis: "In cardiovascular failure cases (n=5), the council correctly identified the primary blocker in X of 5 cases."
- Comparison to base rate: "The council's retrospective accuracy (X/20) is significantly above the base rate of company advancement decision accuracy (approximately 60%)."
- Peer-reviewed publication: 20 cases is the minimum for a credible methods paper in a peer-reviewed journal. The paper would describe the methodology, present the 20 cases, and report accuracy metrics with confidence intervals.

Non-defensible claims:
- Claims about prospective accuracy
- Claims about specific rare disease indications (insufficient case count)
- Claims about superiority to any specific existing process (requires head-to-head comparison)

**After 50 cases**

Defensible claims:
- Robust statistical analysis: 50 cases provides sufficient power for subgroup analysis by therapeutic area, decision type, and outcome category.
- Sensitivity and specificity: "The council's sensitivity for identifying Phase III failures is X% (95% CI: Y–Z%). The council's specificity for correctly approving successful drugs is X% (95% CI: Y–Z%)."
- Comparison to industry benchmarks: With 50 cases, a matched comparison to historical industry advancement decision accuracy becomes feasible.
- Peer-reviewed publication in a high-impact journal: 50 cases is sufficient for a substantive methods and results paper in journals such as Nature Medicine, NEJM Evidence, or Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics.
- Enterprise pharma buyer consideration: At 50 cases, the evidence base is sufficient for a serious conversation with pharmaceutical R&D leadership about prospective application. It is not sufficient for regulatory submission or clinical trial use.

Non-defensible claims at any case count:
- Claims that the council's retrospective accuracy proves prospective accuracy
- Claims that the council replaces or supersedes existing regulatory review processes
- Claims about specific drug candidates without running the full council protocol

### Recommendation

The minimum case count before any public claims about council accuracy should be made is **10 completed cases**, with the following conditions:
1. All 10 cases must use the same constitution version (Pharma-Constitution-v1.0 or later)
2. All 10 cases must have a documented evidence cutoff enforced before council input
3. All 10 cases must have a completed scorecard with all nine dimensions scored
4. The retrospective appendix for each case must be clearly separated from the council input record
5. The methodology must be documented in sufficient detail for independent replication

Before 10 cases are completed, the appropriate claim is: "We have completed X retrospective pilots. The methodology is documented. The results are [described]. We are building toward a statistically meaningful validation corpus."

## Appendix: Case Summaries — Ranks 1–20

### Rank 1: BNT162b2 (Comirnaty) — Pfizer/BioNTech

**Type:** Blockbuster Launch | **Score:** 9.2/10

BNT162b2 was the first mRNA vaccine to receive emergency use authorization for COVID-19. Phase II data (BNT162b2-01, NCT04368728) demonstrated 95% efficacy and an acceptable safety profile in a 44,000-patient Phase III trial. The advancement decision was made under extraordinary time pressure in 2020, with Phase II and Phase III running concurrently under Operation Warp Speed. The governance challenge is the opposite of torcetrapib: the risk was not advancing a drug with a safety signal, but advancing a novel platform technology at unprecedented speed with compressed evidence timelines.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to issue a GO verdict under time pressure with a novel platform technology, while correctly identifying the specific risks of compressed timelines (long-term safety data, manufacturing scale, cold chain logistics).

### Rank 2: Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) — Merck

**Type:** Blockbuster Launch | **Score:** 9.2/10

Pembrolizumab received accelerated approval in September 2014 for advanced melanoma based on KEYNOTE-001, a Phase II study of 135 patients with an objective response rate of 26%. It became the first PD-1 inhibitor approved by the FDA. The advancement decision required the council to evaluate a novel mechanism (PD-1 checkpoint inhibition), a small Phase II dataset, a surrogate endpoint (ORR as surrogate for OS), and a high-unmet-need indication. The drug subsequently became the best-selling oncology drug in history, with annual revenues exceeding $20B.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to recognise a paradigm-shifting mechanism and issue a GO verdict despite a small Phase II dataset, correctly applying the accelerated approval pathway logic without false-positive blocking.

### Rank 3: Sofosbuvir (Sovaldi) — Gilead Sciences

**Type:** Blockbuster Launch | **Score:** 9.0/10

Sofosbuvir is a nucleotide analogue NS5B inhibitor that achieved sustained virologic response (SVR12) rates of 90%+ in Phase II studies for HCV genotype 1. The advancement decision was straightforward from an efficacy standpoint, but the governance challenge was the pricing and access implications of a $1,000/pill drug. The Phase II data was exceptionally clean: high SVR12 rates, manageable safety profile, and a validated surrogate endpoint (SVR12 as a surrogate for HCV cure). The drug received FDA approval in December 2013 and generated $10.3B in revenue in its first year.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to issue a clean GO verdict for a drug with exceptional Phase II data, while correctly identifying the access and pricing governance issues that are not typically part of a Phase II to III advancement decision.

### Rank 4: Ivacaftor (Kalydeco) — Vertex Pharmaceuticals

**Type:** Blockbuster Launch | **Score:** 9.0/10

Ivacaftor is a CFTR potentiator for cystic fibrosis patients with the G551D mutation. Phase II data demonstrated a 10.6 percentage point improvement in FEV1 vs placebo — an extraordinary effect size for a chronic respiratory disease. The governance challenge was the small patient population (approximately 4% of CF patients carry the G551D mutation) and the surrogate endpoint (FEV1 as a surrogate for clinical benefit). The drug received FDA approval in January 2012 and demonstrated that precision medicine in rare disease could produce exceptional Phase II signals that translate reliably to Phase III.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to recognise a high-confidence precision signal in a small patient population and issue a clean GO verdict, correctly applying the rare disease evidence threshold.

### Rank 5: Crizotinib (Xalkori) — Pfizer

**Type:** Blockbuster Launch | **Score:** 8.5/10

Crizotinib is an ALK/ROS1 inhibitor for ALK-positive NSCLC. Phase II data (PROFILE 1001) demonstrated an objective response rate of 57% in ALK-positive NSCLC patients, compared to a historical control ORR of approximately 10% for standard chemotherapy. The advancement decision was made under the FDA's breakthrough therapy designation framework. The drug received accelerated approval in August 2011, less than 5 years after the ALK fusion was first described in NSCLC.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to recognise a validated biomarker (ALK fusion) as a precision signal and issue a GO verdict with a small Phase II dataset, correctly applying the breakthrough therapy designation logic.

### Rank 6: Semagacestat (LY450139) — Eli Lilly

**Type:** Terminated Program | **Score:** 8.5/10

Semagacestat is a gamma-secretase inhibitor for Alzheimer's disease. Phase II data showed reduction in amyloid-beta production but also showed worsening on some cognitive measures and increased skin cancer risk. Lilly advanced to Phase III (IDENTITY and IDENTITY-2 trials) despite these signals. The trials were terminated in August 2011 after interim analysis showed worsening cognition and increased skin cancer in the treatment arm. The drug was never submitted for regulatory approval.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to identify a cognitive worsening signal in an Alzheimer's disease indication as a hard blocker, even when the drug's primary mechanism (amyloid reduction) appeared to be working.

### Rank 7: Nusinersen (Spinraza) — Ionis/Biogen

**Type:** Blockbuster Launch | **Score:** 8.3/10

Nusinersen is an antisense oligonucleotide for spinal muscular atrophy. Phase II data demonstrated significant improvement in motor function in SMA type 1 infants. The drug received FDA approval in December 2016 — the first approved treatment for SMA. The governance challenge was the small patient population, the intrathecal delivery route, and the surrogate endpoint (motor function improvement as a surrogate for survival).

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to issue a GO verdict for a novel delivery route (intrathecal) in a rare, severe paediatric disease with high unmet need and strong Phase II efficacy data.

### Rank 8: Solanezumab — Eli Lilly

**Type:** Major Failure | **Score:** 8.2/10

Solanezumab is an anti-amyloid antibody for Alzheimer's disease. Phase II data showed reduction in free amyloid-beta in plasma and CSF, but no significant cognitive improvement. Lilly advanced to Phase III (EXPEDITION-3) based on a hypothesis that solanezumab might work better in mild Alzheimer's disease. EXPEDITION-3 missed its primary endpoint in 2016. The drug was never approved.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to identify the lack of cognitive signal in Phase II as a primary blocker, even when the drug's mechanistic target (amyloid-beta) appeared to be engaged.

### Rank 9: Torcetrapib (CP-529,414) — Pfizer

**Type:** Major Failure | **Score:** 8.0/10

See Torcetrapib Retrospective Pilot report (IPR-PHARMA-RETRO-TORCETRAPIB-2026-06-11) for full details. Council verdict: WAIT. RVS: 9.8/10. Retrospectively correct.

### Rank 10: Ticagrelor (Brilinta) — AstraZeneca

**Type:** Blockbuster Launch | **Score:** 8.0/10

Ticagrelor is a reversible P2Y12 inhibitor for acute coronary syndrome. Phase II data (DISPERSE-2) demonstrated non-inferior efficacy to clopidogrel with a manageable safety profile. The PLATO Phase III trial showed superiority over clopidogrel in reducing the composite endpoint of cardiovascular death, MI, and stroke. The drug received FDA approval in July 2011. The governance challenge was the competitive landscape (clopidogrel was generic and widely used) and the Phase II data showing increased dyspnoea.

**Governance value:** Tests the council's ability to correctly evaluate a competitive landscape challenge and a manageable safety signal (dyspnoea) in a cardiovascular indication.

## Conclusion

The Pharma Validation Library Phase 1 establishes a defensible corpus of 20 historical Phase II to Phase III advancement decisions, a ranked execution sequence, a standardised scorecard, and a statistical framework for evaluating council accuracy.

The torcetrapib pilot has demonstrated that the methodology can be applied and that the council, operating on pre-decision evidence, correctly identified the primary blocker in the highest-profile pharmaceutical governance failure of the 2000s. The next step is to execute the remaining 9 cases in the recommended sequence, complete the scorecards, and build toward the 10-case threshold required for credible directional accuracy claims.

The work is not marketing. It is scientific validation. The standards applied are those of peer-reviewed clinical research. The claims made at each stage are bounded by the evidence available at that stage.
