## PART 1 — DEMO NARRATIVE

### Design Principle

The demo is not a product tour. It is a structured argument. The argument is: *"A structured, multi-persona governance council, operating on pre-decision evidence only, would have identified the risk that killed a $21 billion drug program — before Pfizer spent $800 million on Phase III."* Every minute of the demo is in service of that argument. The product is the proof.

The demo uses one case only: torcetrapib. It does not show the full 10-case library in detail. The library is referenced but not walked through. One case, done completely, is more persuasive than ten cases done superficially.

### Minute-by-Minute Agenda

---

**Minutes 0:00–2:00 — Opening**

*Presenter action:* Open with the case library screen. Do not introduce the company. Do not explain the technology. Open with the data.

*Opening line:* "Before we begin, I want to show you something. This is our 10-case retrospective validation library. Each row is a real Phase II → III advancement decision. Each row has a council verdict and a historical outcome. I want to walk you through one of them — the one that cost Pfizer $800 million and $21 billion in market cap."

*Screen:* Case Library View (Screen 1). Torcetrapib row is highlighted. All other rows visible but not in focus.

*Purpose:* Establish that this is evidence-based, not a pitch. The audience is looking at data before they hear a single claim.

---

**Minutes 2:00–5:00 — The Problem**

*Presenter action:* Navigate to the Decision Brief screen for torcetrapib. Read the brief aloud, slowly.

*Key message:* "In 2005, Pfizer had a drug that raised HDL cholesterol by 72% and lowered LDL by 25%. The mechanism was novel — CETP inhibition. The Phase II data looked extraordinary. The commercial opportunity was enormous — potentially the largest cardiovascular drug in history. And there was one signal. A blood pressure increase of 2 mmHg systolic. Small. Unexplained. Possibly off-target. Pfizer advanced to Phase III."

*Screen:* Decision Brief View (Screen 2). Evidence cutoff date visible: November 30, 2005. Admitted evidence list visible.

*Pause:* "The question we asked was: if a structured governance council had reviewed this decision in 2005 — with only the evidence available at that time — what would they have said?"

*Purpose:* Create the tension. The audience knows what happened. They don't yet know what the council said.

---

**Minutes 5:00–12:00 — The Council Execution**

*Presenter action:* Navigate to the Council Chamber screen. Walk through the vote distribution.

*Key message:* "The council has 10 personas. Each one represents a distinct governance perspective — the Chief Biostatistician, the Drug Safety Expert, the Regulatory Strategist, and seven others. Each persona reviewed the same evidence. Each voted independently. Here is what they said."

*Screen:* Council Chamber View (Screen 3). Vote distribution visible: 0 GO / 8 WAIT / 2 NO-GO. Walk through three personas in detail.

*Persona 1 — Drug Safety Expert (NO-GO, 95% confidence):* "The blood pressure signal is unexplained. A 2 mmHg increase in a cardiovascular drug is not a rounding error. It is a signal that requires a mechanistic explanation before 15,000 patients are enrolled in Phase III. I cannot vote GO on an unexplained safety signal in a cardiovascular indication."

*Persona 2 — Chief Biostatistician (WAIT, 95% confidence):* "The Phase II trial was powered for lipid endpoints, not cardiovascular events. The blood pressure finding is a secondary observation in an underpowered study. We do not know if it is real, dose-dependent, or mechanism-specific. We need a mechanistic study before Phase III."

*Persona 3 — Portfolio Manager (WAIT, 90% confidence):* "The commercial opportunity is real. But the risk-adjusted value of advancing with an unexplained safety signal is negative. If the signal is real and mechanism-specific, Phase III will fail. The expected value of a 6-month mechanistic study is positive. We should wait."

*Purpose:* Show the council reasoning, not just the vote. The audience needs to see that the council is not a black box.

---

**Minutes 12:00–16:00 — The Governance Layer**

*Presenter action:* Navigate to the Constitution Audit screen. Show the rules that were invoked.

*Key message:* "The council doesn't just vote. It operates under a governance constitution — 10 rules that define what evidence is required before a Phase III advancement can be approved. Here are the three rules that blocked torcetrapib."

*Screen:* Constitution Audit View (Screen 4). Three rules highlighted.

*Rule PC-001:* "Phase II safety signals must be mechanistically investigated before Phase III advancement is authorised. The blood pressure signal was not mechanistically investigated."

*Rule PC-003:* "Financial pressure must be documented and disclosed to the council. The council noted that Pfizer's $800M investment and Lipitor patent cliff created advancement pressure. This does not change the vote, but it is documented."

*Rule PC-007:* "Surrogate endpoints must be validated as predictors of clinical outcomes before Phase III advancement. HDL-C elevation had not been validated as a cardiovascular event predictor at the time of the decision."

*Purpose:* Show that the verdict is rule-based, not opinion-based. This is the governance layer that makes the output auditable.

---

**Minutes 16:00–19:00 — The Output**

*Presenter action:* Navigate to the Institutional Proof Report screen. Show the report structure.

*Key message:* "The council produces an Institutional Proof Report. It is not a recommendation. It is a governance record. It documents what evidence was reviewed, what rules were applied, what each persona said, and what the final verdict was. It is designed to be reviewed by a regulatory authority, a board, or a diligence team."

*Screen:* Institutional Proof Report View (Screen 5). Show: verdict (WAIT), proof score (93/100), key blockers, constitutional rules invoked, evidence boundary statement.

*Then navigate to Outcome Comparison View (Screen 6):* "This is what happened. ILLUMINATE was terminated December 2, 2006. 82 deaths versus 51 in the control arm. The blood pressure signal grew from 2 mmHg in Phase II to 5.4 mmHg in Phase III. The off-target aldosterone mechanism was confirmed in 2008. The council's WAIT verdict was retrospectively correct."

*Purpose:* Close the argument. The council identified the risk. The risk was real. The verdict was correct.

---

**Minutes 19:00–22:00 — The Pilot Proposal**

*Presenter action:* Navigate to the Pilot Proposal screen. Transition from demo to conversation.

*Key message:* "We are not asking you to deploy this in your Phase III advancement process. We are asking you to pick one historical case from your own pipeline — a drug that advanced to Phase III in the last 10 years — and let us run the council on it. Thirty days. Public data only. $25,000. At the end, you have an Institutional Proof Report for your own case. You decide whether it is useful."

*Screen:* Pilot Proposal View (Screen 7). Scope, deliverables, timeline, price visible.

*Purpose:* Make the ask specific, bounded, and low-risk.

---

**Minutes 22:00–30:00 — Q&A**

*Presenter action:* Stop presenting. Ask one question: "What case would you want us to run?"

*Purpose:* The best outcome of the demo is not a signed agreement. It is the prospect naming a case. A named case means scientific interest. Scientific interest is the prerequisite for a pilot.

## PART 3 — EXECUTIVE VIEW (CEO)

A CEO attending this demo has one question: *"Is this real, and does it matter?"*

They are not evaluating the methodology. They are evaluating the claim. The claim is: a structured AI governance council would have prevented a $21 billion loss. The CEO's job is to determine whether that claim is credible enough to warrant further investigation.

**What a CEO sees in the first 5 minutes:** The case library table. Ten cases. A clear verdict column. A clear outcome column. A clear alignment column. Running metrics: 7 correct, 9 aligned, 0 false positives. The CEO does not need to understand CETP inhibition or Phase II statistics. They need to see that the system has a track record, that the track record is documented, and that the track record includes the right kind of cases — major failures and major successes.

**What a CEO hears that matters:** "Pfizer had a signal. The signal was small. The financial pressure was enormous. The council said wait. Pfizer advanced. The signal was real. $800 million and $21 billion later, the mechanism was confirmed. The council was right." This is a CEO-level story. It does not require pharmaceutical expertise to understand.

**What a CEO asks:** "How do I know the AI didn't already know the answer?" This is the most important CEO question. The answer is: "We can't prove it didn't. We've disclosed that limitation in writing. Here is what we're doing about it. Here is why we believe the council reasoning is still valuable even with that limitation." The CEO will respect the honesty. They will not respect a claim of certainty.

**What a CEO needs to see to approve a pilot:** A specific, bounded scope. A fixed price. A clear deliverable. A named case that is relevant to their pipeline. The pilot proposal screen answers all four.

**CEO-specific talking point:** "The question is not whether AI can replace your governance process. It can't. The question is whether a structured, documented, multi-perspective council review — run in parallel with your existing process — would catch risks that your current process misses. One retrospective case will tell you whether the answer is yes."

## PART 4 — CLINICAL VIEW (HEAD OF CLINICAL DEVELOPMENT)

A Head of Clinical Development has a different question: *"Is this methodology scientifically credible?"*

They will scrutinise the evidence manifest. They will challenge the persona reasoning. They will ask about the constitutional rules. They will want to know who wrote the rules and whether they reflect actual pharmaceutical development practice.

**What a Head of Clinical Development sees:** The evidence manifest is the most important screen for this audience. They will read the admitted documents list and spot-check the exclusion list. If the manifest is specific (DOIs, publication dates, access dates), they will respect it. If it is vague, they will dismiss it.

**What a Head of Clinical Development hears that matters:** The persona reasoning must be in pharmaceutical development language. "The Phase II trial was powered for lipid endpoints, not cardiovascular events" is credible. "The AI identified a risk factor" is not. The Chief Biostatistician and Drug Safety Expert persona cards must read like they were written by pharmaceutical development professionals, because the Head of Clinical Development will evaluate them against that standard.

**What a Head of Clinical Development challenges:** "The blood pressure signal was known. Everyone knew about it. Pfizer knew about it. The question was whether it was clinically significant." The response: "You are correct. The signal was known. The council's contribution is not identifying the signal — it is documenting the governance failure: the signal was known, it was unexplained, and it was not mechanistically investigated before Phase III advancement. The constitutional rule PC-001 requires that investigation. It was not done. That is the governance finding."

**What a Head of Clinical Development needs to see to approve a pilot:** A case from their own pipeline where the governance question is genuinely uncertain. Not a famous failure where everyone knows the answer. A case where the council might produce a finding that is not obvious — and where the Head of Clinical Development is curious enough about the answer to spend $25,000 finding out.

**Clinical-specific talking point:** "We are not telling you what the right decision was. We are documenting what the governance process should have required — and whether it did. The output is a governance record, not a clinical recommendation. Your clinical team makes the decision. The council documents whether the governance process was followed."

## PART 5 — REGULATORY VIEW (REGULATORY AFFAIRS LEADER)

A Regulatory Affairs leader has a third question: *"Does this create regulatory risk or reduce it?"*

Their primary concern is that the council output could be discoverable in litigation or regulatory proceedings, or that it could create a paper trail that contradicts the company's actual decision-making process.

**What a Regulatory Affairs leader sees:** The Institutional Proof Report structure is the most important element for this audience. They will read the audit references section carefully. They will ask about document retention, discoverability, and the relationship between the council output and the company's formal governance documentation.

**What a Regulatory Affairs leader hears that matters:** "The pilot uses only public data. The council output is a governance analysis document, not a regulatory submission. It is not a clinical study report. It is not a safety report. It is not a document that would be submitted to FDA or EMA. Its regulatory status is the same as any internal governance analysis document — subject to your company's document retention and legal hold policies."

**What a Regulatory Affairs leader challenges:** "If we run this on a drug that is currently in our pipeline, and the council issues a NO-GO verdict, does that create a liability?" The response: "That is a question for your legal team, not ours. We recommend that the pilot use a historical case — a drug that has already completed Phase III — for exactly this reason. The retrospective pilot creates no forward-looking liability because the decision has already been made."

**What a Regulatory Affairs leader needs to see to approve a pilot:** A clear statement that the pilot uses only public data, that the output is an internal governance document, and that AgenThink does not retain or disclose the output without the client's consent. The DPA template addresses all three.

**Regulatory-specific talking point:** "The evidence boundary statement is the most important document in the package for your purposes. It documents exactly what evidence was used, when it was cut off, and what was excluded. If the output is ever reviewed, the evidence boundary statement demonstrates that the council operated on pre-decision information only — the same standard that applies to any retrospective governance analysis."

## PART 6 — CRO PARTNER VIEW (ICON / MEDPACE)

A CRO executive — whether from ICON, Medpace, Labcorp, or Parexel — has a different frame entirely. They are not evaluating the council as a buyer. They are evaluating it as a potential partner or competitive threat. Their question is: *"Is this something we should offer our clients, or something that competes with our governance services?"*

**What a CRO executive sees:** The pilot proposal screen is the most important element for this audience. They are looking at the $25,000 price point and the 30-day timeline and asking: "Could we white-label this? Could we offer this as an add-on to our Phase II/III transition services? Could we build this ourselves?"

**What a CRO executive hears that matters:** The framing for a CRO is partnership, not competition. "We are not a CRO. We do not run clinical trials. We do not manage Phase III programs. We build the governance layer that sits above the clinical execution. Your clients trust you with the execution. We are asking whether they should also trust a structured governance council with the advancement decision." The CRO executive's interest is in whether the council can be bundled with their existing services to increase the value proposition to their clients.

**What a CRO executive challenges:** "We already provide governance advisory services. Our medical directors review Phase II data and advise on Phase III design. How is this different?" The response: "Your medical directors are advisors. They provide opinions. The council provides a structured, documented, multi-perspective governance record that is auditable, reproducible, and independent of the clinical team. The difference is not the quality of the analysis — it is the governance architecture. Your medical director's opinion is not a governance record. The council output is."

**What a CRO executive needs to see to engage:** A partnership model that is additive to their existing services, not competitive. The most natural partnership structure is: the CRO provides the clinical execution and the Phase II data package; AgenThink provides the governance council layer; the combined output is a Phase III readiness package that includes both the clinical design recommendation and the governance council verdict. The CRO bills the client for the combined package.

## PART 7 — PILOT PROPOSAL

### The Transition

The transition from demo to pilot proposal happens at minute 19. It is not a sales close. It is a question. The question is: "What case would you want us to run?"

This question does three things simultaneously. It makes the ask specific (a case, not a contract). It makes the ask low-risk (one case, 30 days, $25,000). And it reveals the prospect's level of scientific interest. A prospect who names a case is a prospect who is genuinely curious about the answer. A prospect who deflects to procurement is a prospect who is interested but not yet convinced.

### The Pilot Structure

**Scope:** One historical Phase II → III advancement decision. The case must be from the prospect's own pipeline or from a therapeutic area where the prospect has deep expertise. The case must have a clear Phase III outcome (approved, failed, terminated, or withdrawn). Public data only for the retrospective pilot.

**Timeline:** 30 days from signed agreement to delivered Institutional Proof Report.

| Day | Activity |
|---|---|
| Day 1–3 | Case selection confirmed. Evidence cutoff date agreed. |
| Day 4–10 | Evidence corpus built by AgenThink Evidence Curator. |
| Day 11–12 | Evidence corpus reviewed and approved by client expert (2–4 hours of client time). |
| Day 13 | Evidence corpus locked. Council run executed. |
| Day 14–20 | Institutional Proof Report drafted. |
| Day 21–25 | Internal review. Client preview call. |
| Day 26–28 | Final report delivered. |
| Day 29–30 | Debrief meeting. Pilot evaluation. |

**Deliverables:**

1. Evidence Manifest — complete list of admitted documents with DOIs and cutoff verification
2. Council Output JSON — machine-readable full council result
3. Institutional Proof Report PDF — 4–6 pages, full governance record
4. Governance Audit Log — session ID, model version, constitution version, hashes
5. Debrief presentation — 15-minute walkthrough of findings and implications

**Pricing:** $25,000 fixed fee. No variable components. No success fees. No ongoing commitment.

**IP:** AgenThink retains methodology IP. Client retains any proprietary data provided (none in retrospective pilot). Joint ownership of the specific pilot output — the Institutional Proof Report for the client's case.

**Confidentiality:** Mutual NDA. AgenThink does not disclose the client's case selection, the evidence corpus, or the pilot output without the client's written consent. AgenThink may reference the existence of a pilot (not the content) in its marketing materials, subject to client approval.

### The Free First Run Option

For prospects where scientific interest is high but procurement friction is significant (Roche, Novartis, AstraZeneca), offer one free demo run on a case of their choosing — using only public data, with a 2-week turnaround instead of 30 days. The free run produces a Council Output JSON and a summary report (not the full Institutional Proof Report). The purpose is to demonstrate the methodology on the prospect's own case before asking for a commercial commitment.

The free run is not a pilot. It is a proof of concept for the proof of concept. It is offered only when the scientific interest is clear and the commercial barrier is procurement process, not scientific credibility.

### Handling the Procurement Objection

The most common reason a pilot does not close after a successful demo is procurement process. The prospect is interested. Their legal team requires a vendor assessment. Their IT team requires a security review. Their procurement team requires three competing bids. The pilot agreement is in legal review for 8 weeks.

The mitigation is: have the pilot agreement template ready before the meeting. At the end of the demo, offer to send the template directly to the prospect's legal team. "We have a standard pilot agreement. It is four pages. It has been reviewed by our legal counsel. If you can get it to your legal team this week, we can typically turn around comments in 48 hours." Speed in the legal process is a competitive advantage.

## PART 9 — SUCCESS CRITERIA

### How We Know the Demo Worked

A demo is not successful because the prospect was polite. A demo is not successful because the prospect said "interesting." A demo is successful when it produces one of three outcomes.

**Outcome A — Named Case (Best):** The prospect names a case they would want the council to run. "We had a drug in 2018 that failed Phase III. I'd be curious what the council would have said." A named case means scientific interest. Scientific interest is the prerequisite for a pilot. Probability of pilot from named case: 60–75%.

**Outcome B — Follow-Up Meeting (Good):** The prospect requests a follow-up meeting with a specific agenda — typically a deeper methodology review with their scientific team, or a legal review of the pilot agreement. A follow-up meeting with a specific agenda means the prospect is moving through their evaluation process. Probability of pilot from follow-up meeting: 30–50%.

**Outcome C — Document Request (Acceptable):** The prospect requests specific documents — the methodology paper, the GitHub link, the pilot agreement template, the evidence manifest for torcetrapib. A document request means the prospect is doing due diligence. Probability of pilot from document request: 15–25%.

**Outcome D — No Response (Failure):** The prospect thanks you for the demo and does not follow up. This is a failure. It means the demo did not produce sufficient scientific interest to motivate further action. The most common cause is a mismatch between the demo content and the prospect's specific governance concerns. The fix is to research the prospect's pipeline and tailor the case selection before the next demo.

### Quantitative Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| Named case rate | ≥ 50% of demos | Tracked in CRM |
| Follow-up meeting rate | ≥ 70% of demos | Tracked in CRM |
| Demo-to-pilot conversion | ≥ 25% of demos | Tracked in CRM |
| Demo duration (actual vs. planned) | ≤ 35 minutes | Tracked in meeting notes |
| Objections raised per demo | 2–5 (healthy) | Tracked in meeting notes |
| Objections not answered satisfactorily | 0 | Tracked in meeting notes |

### Post-Demo Protocol

Within 24 hours of every demo, the presenter sends a follow-up email containing: (a) a thank-you for the meeting; (b) the three documents most relevant to the questions raised in the demo; (c) a specific next step with a deadline ("I'll send the pilot agreement template by Thursday; if you can get it to your legal team by Friday, we can start the pilot on July 14"); (d) a one-sentence summary of the case the prospect mentioned, if any.

The follow-up email is not a sales email. It is a service email. It provides value — specific documents that answer specific questions — before asking for anything.

### The One Question That Determines Demo Quality

After every demo, the presenter asks themselves one question: *"Did the prospect name a case?"*

If yes: the demo worked. Follow up within 24 hours with the pilot agreement template.

If no: the demo did not work. Diagnose why. Was the case (torcetrapib) relevant to the prospect's therapeutic area? Was the evidence boundary explanation clear? Was the pilot proposal specific enough? Adjust for the next demo.

The named case is the only metric that matters in the first 30 days of outreach.
